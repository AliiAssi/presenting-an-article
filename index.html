  • Methodology
    • way to estimate a company’s cybersecurity risk
    • Sectoral Risk Modeling Approach 1
      • risk here are modeling by 3 factors: Proba, Impact, Defense Gap Index
      • formula: R = P * I * G
      • P based on the average incident rate across the industry
      • I model uses the average cost of a cyber incident across the peer group
      • G is the key innovation in this contribution
        • measures how much a company’s security posture deviates from the industry average.
        • if the single firm has weaker security than the peer → G = 1 (HIGH RISK)
        • else G < 1 (LOW RISK)
        • G is calculated based on how much a firm's security posture deviates from the industry average.
      • after this method
        • answering the first question , calculating the risk, and based on firm data, firms can estimate their expected loss
        • answering the second question, by calculating the DGI ,allows firms to benchmark their security posture against others in the industry.
    • Sectoral Risk Modeling Approach 2
      • answer the question: How bad could a cyber incident be?
      • Simulating Cyber Losses with Monte Carlo
        • Monte Carlo simulations generate thousands of possible cyber loss scenarios based on real-world data from industry peers.
        • highlights 2 types of risks: rare but high-impact && frequent but small losses
        • each type is modeled separately using
          • past incidents (historical data)
          • estimating likely losses (predictions)
        • a computer to runs millions of random simulations to predict different cyber loss sinario
      • Creating a Loss Exceedance Curve
        • kind of regression or a risk-gbt
        • answering the user “How much risk should we prepare for?” or “What’s the chance my company will lose more than $500,000 in a single attack?”
        • helps companies understand worst-case scenarios.
        • While Approach 1 (PLG = R) helps firms compare risk with peers, Approach 2 (Monte Carlo) provides a big-picture view of how extreme cyber losses could be for the entire industry.
    • The two approaches are complementary, meaning they can be used together for a more complete risk assessment, but they can also be applied separately, depending on the specific problem.
    • if a company wants to understand its own risk and compare it with peers →Use Approach 1 (PLG = R)
    • If a company, insurer, or policymaker wants to understand the probability of extreme losses → Use Approach 2 (Monte Carlo)
    • For a full cyber risk analysis , using both approaches together gives the most comprehensive results.
  • limitations
    • Self-reported Data Can Be Inaccurate
      • data used in the secure computations is self-reported by organizations, meaning it may contain errors or inconsistencies.
    • Defense Gap Index (DGI) Calculation is Approximate
      • The DGI Calculation involves some subjective decisions, like how much weight to assign to different security controls.
    • Limited Number of Participants
      • only 25 firms in one industry.
      • A larger sample size would improve accuracy
    • The Model Assumes Similar Threats Across an Industry
      • Approach 1 (PLG = R) assumes that all firms in a sector face similar cyber threats and financial risks.
    • Monte Carlo Simulations Use Limited Data
      • Cyber threats evolve rapidly, and past incidents may not accurately predict future attack patterns.
